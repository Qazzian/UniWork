A very brief look at other design methods:


HOOD: `Hierarchical Object Oriented Design'.

HOOD is in some ways an alternative to MASCOT but is 'object-based'.

It has its origins in France, for the European Space Agency. It is/was used in the EFA (Eurofighter) project.

Had a mixed reception in the software engineering community.

The system is decomposed into objects.

Objects are normally related to system functions - i.e. functional decomposition is used.

Parent objects decompose into child objects.

There are active objects and passive objects. The active objects contain a concurrent thread of execution. Passive objects do not - they provide a service via ADTs and their associated operations.

Objects call other objects. Active objects can call active or passive objects; passive objects can only call other passive objects.

HOOD is hierarchical and two hierarchies are represented in the diagrams:

  • the 'uses' hierarchy
  • the functional decomposition.

HOOD is diagrammatic, but does not explicitly represent concurrency in the way that MASCOT does; indications of concurrency are via the existence of the active objects.

There is also a textual representation.

Concurrent objects can communicate directly, not via (explicit) shared data areas, but HOOD is used with Ada and IPC uses the Ada rendezvous.

Unlike MASCOT, HOOD diagrams represent execution control information such as interrupts, timeouts, (Ada) exceptions.

The diagrammatic representation in HOOD is useful for representing the outcome of a design process; it is not that good for progressively developing a design.

MASCOT provides a better visualisation of what goes on inside a concurrent system.

A more recent development of HOOD is HRT HOOD.

This improves its use as a progressive design tool.

Useful overviews of HOOD are provided in the books by Budgen and Cooling (see the book list). A worked example in HRT HOOD is given in the book by Burns and Wellings (Editions 2 and 3).
