AI, Science And Engineering

  • ``Scientists explore what is. Engineers create what has never been'' -- von Kalman.
  • The only uncontroversial ``intelligent agents'' are human beings. For some kinds of problem, how they (or other ``natural problem solvers'') solve them may be the AI engineer's best guide.
  • When considering human information processing, what level should we work at?
  • Other natural problem-solvers: non-human animals, populations of organisms, ant colonies, immune systems, organisations...

Garry Kasparov And Deep Blue

In 1997, world chess champion Kasparov, was defeated in a 6-game match by IBM's ``Deep Blue''. This event, and reactions to it, provide a way into this part of the module.

  • How does Deep Blue choose a move?
  • How does Garry Kasparov choose a move?
  • Is Deep Blue intelligent? Has it passed the Turing Test? Does it really ``play chess''?
  • In 1957, Herbert Simon predicted that within ten years of that date, a machine would be world chess champion. Why was he so wrong?
  • How useful are chess and other ``microworlds'' in AI research?

How Deep Blue Chooses A Move

  • Special purpose hardware.
  • Minimaxing (14-15 ply, with refinements).

How Garry Kasparov Chooses A Move

  • Practice for more than 30 years, building up a repertoire of recognisable patterns.
  • Study the opponent in advance, seeking to understand their style of play.
  • Plan and replan during the game, using your pattern repertoire.
  • Aim to disturb and disconcert the opponent.

The Turing Test For Artificial Intelligence

  • Proposed by mathematician and computer pioneer Alan Turing in 1950.
  • Usually described, inaccurately, as follows: a computer can be regarded as intelligent if an "interrogator" is unable to tell, from its typed responses to questions, that it is a machine rather than a human.
  • As Turing actually posed it, slightly more complicated: can a computer fool the interrogator into thinking it's a woman, as often as a man can?

Deep Blue And The Turing Test

  • Clearly, Deep Blue did not pass (or ``sit'') the Turing Test.
  • Nevertheless, it did appear to do something normally thought to require intelligence. So:
    • Does its ``BFI'' approach undermine its claim to intelligence?
    • Does the fact that it did not know it was playing chess?
    • Is playing (very good) chess sufficient evidence of intelligence?

Formal, Expert and Mundane Tasks

  • Formal tasks include playing board or card games; solving puzzles, and mathematical and logical problems; and programming. They do not require knowledge of the real world. These have turned out to be the easiest tasks for AI.
  • Expert tasks include medical diagnosis, engineering, architectural or computer hardware design, and scheduling (e.g. airline flights, school or university classes, manufacturing processes).
  • Mundane tasks include things we might not think of as tasks or problems at all: understanding everyday speech and written language, making sense of what we see, and walking. These have turned out to be the hardest tasks for AI.

How To Pass The Turing Test

What would an AI program need to do to pass the Turing test?

1.
Understand (typed) natural language.
2.
Produce (typed) natural language.
3.
Understand conversational structure.
4.
Display everyday knowledge.
5.
Display appropriate specialist knowledge.
6.
Model what its interrogator does and does not know.
7.
Model what it does and does not know.

More Rash Predictions

  • ``... within twenty years machines will be capable of doing any work a man can do.'' -- Herbert Simon, 1965.
  • ``I believe that in about fifty years' time it will be possible to programme computers... to play the imitation game so well that an average interrogator will not have more than 70% chance of making the right identification after five minutes of questioning.'' -- Alan Turing, 1950.

Why Will Turing Be Wrong?

Here are several possible answers:

  • Hardware limitations.
  • More knowledge needed than he realised.
  • Better machine learning techniques needed.
  • Intelligence must be based on sensory-motor interaction with the world.
  • Intelligence requires social interaction.
  • Intelligence requires something which is present in human brains, but not in silicon chips.

Microworlds and World Knowledge

  • ``Microworlds'': self-contained, limited range of possible actions, and requirements for knowledge.
  • Much early AI work on them, using general techniques of search and matching.
  • Problems with ``scaling up'' to more realistic domains.
  • General conclusion: need for domain-specific knowledge. Microworlds are useless.
  • Minority opinion (Hofstadter). Knowledge is not enough. We have scarcely begun to understand intelligence. Microworlds a vital research tool.

Lecture 1: Main Points

  • AI has made substantial progress, but far less than most of the ``pioneers'' expected.
  • Tasks thought to be at the pinnacle of intelligence (e.g. chess) turn out to be (relatively!) easy, compared to ``mundane'' tasks (holding a conversation, seeing, sweeping floors). General, flexible artificial intelligence still far off.
  • The best route towards such full-scale AI (and even whether it is possible) remain open questions.
