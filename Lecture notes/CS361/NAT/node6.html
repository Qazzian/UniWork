Michael A. Arbib

  • Arbib has been working on brain theory, artificial intelligence and related areas since the 1960s.
  • He distinguishes neural computing (or neural engineering on the one hand from computational neuroscience on the other, though regarding them as closely related.
  • Neural computing is the branch of artificial intelligence using approaches inspired by human and other animal brains.
  • Computational neuroscience is the use of computer models to advance understanding of how brains work.

The Brain As A Machine

  • Like artificial machines, the brain has a function: enabling its possessor to adapt its behaviour to the environment in order to achieve its goals.
  • Arbib stresses the structural and functional hierarchies found in animal brains and behaviours.
  • He also stresses the close relation between perception and action.
  • The brain is a network of neurons, coupled to receptors and effectors, thus linking perception to action.

Structure And Function Both structure and function of the brain can be analysed at a number of levels.

Organisation Of The Brain

  • Different regions of the brain deal with different functions.
  • Within a region, different layers deal with different levels of analysis of sensory input, and of control of movement.
  • Positions of neurons within a layer usually have a ``maplike'' relationship to their function (e.g. the part of the body surface or visual field they are concerned with).
  • ``The brain is a layered, somatotopic distributed computer.''

Properties of Neurons

  • At rest, there is a voltage difference (polarization) across a neuron's surface membrane.
  • The neuron ``fires'' if its ``axon hillock'' depolarizes far enough, to a threshold level. At this level, depolarization becomes self-sustaining.
  • A ``pulse'' then passes along the axon, to ``synaptic endbulbs'', which contact other neurons.
  • Depending on the type of synapse, it may either excite or inhibit these neurons.

The Importance Of Timing

  • A neuron may have excitatory and inhibitory inputs from many others.
  • It may also have an intrinsic firing rate even without inputs.
  • The geometry of the cell, and the precise relative timing of inputs, rather than just their average rates, may determine whether and when the neuron fires.
  • This could (for example) allow a single cell to act as a motion detector.

Schemas

  • Arbib views the behaviour of an organism, like its brain, as hierarchically organized, but the two do not map directly onto each other.
  • He calls the functional units schemas (compare Schank's scripts, Minsky's frames). Schemas have activation levels, and may compete and/or cooperate.
  • A perceptual schema is concerned with the recognition of some state of the world relevant to the organism. A motor schema controls a particular kind of action.
  • Given a schema-level model, we may look for neural networks able to implement the individual schemas.

Example: The Computational Frog

  • Arbib builds multilevel computational models of frogs' brains and behaviour.
  • A frog snaps at small objects (that might be insects, etc.), and jumps away if something large looms over it. Here's a possible schema-level model of this behaviour.

Testing Schema-Level Models

Such models can be tested experimentally:

  • By varying the environment (what happens if a ``bug'' and an ``enemy'' appear at once?).
  • By ``lesion studies'' (giving the frog brain damage) -- if you think you know which brain region deals with which schema.

Which Way To Jump

  • If the enemy is coming straight toward it, a frog will jump in a direction somewhere between directly away from the enemy, and its current heading.
  • But if the enemy is on course to pass in front of the frog, it will jump away from where the enemy is heading.

Neural Network Models

  • This can be modelled using several sets of neurons, three of which we mention here (the first two visual, the third motor).
    • Each of the first (T3) set responds most strongly to a large object centered on a particular point in visual field.
    • Each of the second (T2) set responds most strongly to such an object moving from edge to centre of visual field.
    • Each of the third (MH) set makes the frog jump in a different direction.
  • Excitation of an MH from a T3 can be countered by inhibition from the corresponding T2, which will also excite a different MH. (Note: Fig.5 in the photocopied paper is misleading.)

From Frogs To Robots

  • Arbib has used his model of ``frog looming perception'' as the basis of an approach to robot obstacle detection and detour path choice.
  • Main difference: there may be several obstacles at once. ``Looming detector'' cells are connected to cells in the ``motor map'' so that they inhibit those corresponding to the same direction, and excite those some distance away.
  • Motor cells corresponding to directions nearer straight ahead have a higher background activity, so they are more likely to be chosen.

Three Principles

1.
Cooperative computation of schemas. Carried out by distributed methods not involving explicit symbolic control: basis not only of action, but of planning and language.
2.
Evolution and modulation. New schemas often arise as ``modulators'' of existing ones, rather than as new systems. Examples: adding T2 ``motion detectors'' to ``looming perception''; adding barrier-detouring schemas to prey-capture schema; adding language to action-based perception.
3.
Interaction of partial representations. No one place in the brain where a complete representation of the world links perception and action.

From Instinctive To Reflective

  • Frogs' schemas are mostly ``hardwired''. They react adaptively, but don't plan ahead.
  • Evolutionary pressure for flexibility has led to goal oriented, reflective behaviour and explicit representation of goals.
  • Human ability to use symbolic representations is most recent development in this direction.
  • Interaction of these two approaches vital to human performance.
  • So why not to artificial intelligences?

Schemas and Schema Instances

  • VISIONS computer vision system is based on use of schemas.
  • Schemas reside in LTM, schema instances in STM.
  • A schema instance is active in directing processing, cooperates and competes with others, is associated with an area of the image being interpreted.
  • Planning is distributed -- result of activity emerging in a flexible network. (Compare with Hofstadter!)

From Biology to Technology

  • In computational neuroscience, we know that eventually, behaviour must be accounted for in terms of the properties of neurons and neural networks.
  • In software engineering, we can choose our hardware.
