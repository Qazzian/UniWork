Intelligence is Social

  • Almost all the most intelligent non-human animals are highly social (most primates, dogs, dolphins, parrots...).
  • Individual humans are no cleverer than 30,000 years ago; yet our collective abilities have expanded enormously.
  • Social insects (bees, wasps, ants, termites) fight off predators, build complex structures, ``domesticate'' or ``enslave'' fungi and other insects...
  • How do human and other animal societies work?

Real Ants

  • Ant colonies are founded by a single queen, but may grow to contain millions of individuals.
  • Some are migratory (e.g. army ants). Most build complex nests.
  • Individual ants cooperate to repel invaders, collect or grow food, rear the young.
  • A biological puzzle: why are ants, bees, wasps, termites and no other insects social?
  • Communication between ants is primarily chemical.

Pheromones

  • Pheromones: chemicals used in animal communication.
  • Ants leave a chemical trail behind them when they forage for food.
  • They also have a tendency to follow the trails laid by their nestmates. Trails therefore tend to grow stronger by an autocatalytic or positive feedback effect.
  • These simple features enable them to use the world as a collective memory bank: this is stigmergy.

Stigmergy

  • Concept introduced by Grasse in 1959, primarily to explain nest-building by social insects.
  • Individual insects modify the environment (add a bit to the nest, lay a trail).
  • These modifications provide new stimuli to all insects that encounter them.
  • These stimuli affect their behavioural responses, which in turn modify the environment further.

Finding Food Sources

  • Ants spread out from their nest, looking for food. Ants finding food return with it.
  • Depending on species, may lay pheromone going out, coming back, or both.
  • In some species, richness of food source found influences amount of pheromone laid.
  • Decision to follow a trail influenced by amount of pheromone, but probabilistic.
  • Also, always a few ants wandering off apparently at random.

Finding Shortest Routes

  • If a source is found by two routes, the shorter will gain an advantage.
  • The system isn't perfect: new shortcuts difficult to find, circular routes can occur.

Ant System

  • Ant System, devised by Alberto Colorni and others for distributed optimization.
  • Based on simple cooperating agents with limited communicative abilities: distinguishes ``ant-based'' from other multi-agent systems.
  • The ``ants'' in Ant System have no idea they are cooperating with other ants, or even that there are other ants.
  • However, they have some features not needed for stigmergy to work: some memory, some ability to sense things other than trail pheromone.
  • Time in the ants ``world'' is discrete.

Travelling SalesAnts: 1

Ant System has been applied to the Travelling Salesman Problem. Initially (at t=0):

  • Ants are distributed evenly among all the ``cities'' to be toured.
  • Low levels of trail pheromone are assigned at random to the ``trail'' between each pair of cities.
  • One of three different ``ant algorithms'' is selected, and various numerical parameters are set by the user.

Ant Algorithms

  • ANT-quantity algorithm: fixed amount Q1 of pheromone is left on a trail that is followed -- so the concentration of pheromone added is greater for closer pairs of cities.
  • ANT-density algorithm: fixed amount Q2 of pheromone is left on every unit of length of a trail that is followed -- so closer pairs of cities are not given the same advantage as in ANT-quantity.
  • ANT-cycle algorithm: pheromone densities not updated after every time-step, but after ants have completed a tour. Fixed amount Q3 of pheromone is spread evenly over complete path of each ant -- so shorter tours contribute greater density of pheromone.

Results

  • For all three algorithms, performance depended strongly on the values of the parameters ( and Q1,Q2 or Q3 depending on the algorithm).
  • Optimal parameters could only be discovered experimentally.
  • ANT-cycle proved significantly more successful than the other two.
  • Using more ants (up to 64) decreased total computation for ANT-quantity and ANT-density, while 16 ants gave better results than more or fewer for ANT-cycle.
  • Ant-system found better results than other approaches tried, but took longer.

Ants On The Line

  • Ruud Schoonderwoerd and others are investigating ant-based control (``ABC'') of load balancing in telecommunications networks, for Hewlett-Packard.
  • Problem: nodes with excess traffic get congested, causing calls to be lost.
  • Communications networks are not built to guarantee successful connections on every call.
  • Number of failures is reduced by routing calls through parts of network with spare capacity: ``load balancing''.
  • Central control of such systems requires links to every part of system, and scales badly.

Network Simulation: 1

  • Simulated network used to test ABC against use of fixed routes and use of more complex ``load balancing agents'', investigated by BT.
  • Each node (switching stations) has:
    • Capacity: number of simultaneous calls allowed.
    • Routing table, with entry for each of the other nodes.
    • Probability of being end node of a call.
    • Current spare capacity.

Network Simulation: 2

  • Ants generated at nodes, with random destinations. At each node, there is a ``pheromone table'' for each destination.
  • Ants move one node per time step, choosing next node probabilistically on basis of destination's pheromone table.
  • Alter pheromone table for their source node at each node they reach.
  • Calls have a source, destination, duration. At each time step, expired calls are removed. New calls routed deterministically using pheromone tables.
  • If any node on route is full, call is lost: % lost calls measures performance.

How Ants find Routes

Aim: to get ants to find short routes, but avoid congestion.

  • Ants lay less pheromone the longer they have been travelling.
  • So those finding short routes to destination tend to have most effect.
  • Ants are delayed at nodes congested with calls -- so at subsequent nodes, they are older and lay less pheromone.
  • Hence, ants and calls dynamically interact with each other.

Ants Versus Load Agents: 1

  • Schoonderwoerd compared ants with use of fixed shortest-path routes, and with use of an alternative type of mobile agent.
  • Alternative ``load agents'' rewrite routing tables for nodes identified as needing traffic management by ``parent agents'' using global data on network traffic.
  • Both ants and load agents able to adapt to network topology, static pattern of call probabilities, and changes in these probabilities.
  • Ants outperformed load agents which outperformed fixed routes.

Ants Versus Load Agents: 2

  • Ants used less bandwidth and switching capacity.
  • ABC system probably more robust: ants do not interfere with each other, and ``damage caused by a corrupt ant will be limited''.
  • Ants likely to need more computation at nodes.
  • Load agents respond faster to changing call probabilities. This only reduces (does not eliminate) ants' performance advantage.
