Lecture 4: Hofstadter's FARG

CS36110, Part 1, Lecture 4:

Doug Hofstadter's

Fluid Analogies Research Group

Simon and Hofstadter

  • Both interested in discovery of patterns (both have worked on extending number-sequences).
  • Simon thinks we're ``nearly there'', Hofstadter thinks we've ``hardly started'', in progress toward genuine AI.
  • Simon thinks human cognition is rather simple, Hofstadter that it's incredibly complex.
  • Simon thinks high-level problem-solving can be understood without understanding how the brain works; Hofstadter doesn't.
  • Simon thinks human intelligence is largely serial; Hofstadter, that it emerges out of multitudes of ``inaccessible'' parallel processes.

Hofstadter's Central Concerns

  • Overblown claims made for many AI programs.
  • Drift of AI away from fundamental issues.
  • Nature of creativity, insight, self-awareness.
  • Relation between perception and ``high-level'' thought.
  • Nature of concepts.
  • Importance of analogies and counterfactuals.
  • Usefulness of microworlds in AI research.

My Summary of Hofstadter's Position

  • Q. Why, after 50 years of AI research, are computers still so stupid?
  • A. Because we still have very little understanding of what makes people intelligent; and what we do have has largely been ignored by most AI researchers.

The ``ELIZA effect''

  • ELIZA: program written by Joseph Weizenbaum in 1964-6.
  • ``Conversed'' with users (via teletype), using sets of simple ``scripts'', the most impressive imitaing a Rogerian therapist.
  • Weizenbaum became alarmed at people's reaction to ELIZA (see Computer Power and Human Reason, 1976).
  • Hofstadter claims that the ``ELIZA effect'' has systematically obstructed objective assessment of AI programs by AI researchers.

Hofstadter's Own List of Themes

  • Inseparability of perception and high-level cognition.
  • Easily reconfigurable, multi-level cognitive representations.
  • ``Subcognitive pressures''.
  • Commingling of many pressures.
  • Simultaneous feeling-out of many potential pathways.
  • Cognitive representations' deeper and shallower layers.
  • Crucial role of inner structure of concepts and conceptual neighbourhoods.

Example Programs

  • Seek-Whence: Extrapolating number sequences.
  • Jumbo: modelling processes involved in finding anagrams.
  • Copycat: constructing analogies (between transformations) in a microworld of letter-sequences.
  • Tabletop: constructing analogies (between actions) in a microworld of ``coffeehouse tabletops''.
  • Letter Spirit: designing typefaces.

Copycat: Overview

  • Written by Hofstadter and Melanie Mitchell (H/M).
  • ``Neither symbolic nor connectionist: ``subcognitive but superneural''.
  • Top-level behaviour emerges from parallel, stochastic processing mechanism. Concepts implemented as distributed and probabilistic entities in a network. (Contrast with GOFAI's ``atomic'' symbols.)
  • Intended to simulate ``the very crux of human cognition: fluid concepts''.
  • Three main components: the ``Slipnet'' (roughly, long-term memory), ``Workspace'' (roughly, short-term memory), and ``Coderack'' (no close counterpart in other architectures).

Copycat: Example Problems

  • abc abd, ijk ?
  • aabc aabd, ijkk ?
  • abc abd, kji ?
  • abc abd, mrrjjj ?
  • abc abd, rssttt ?
  • abc abd, xyz ?

Copycat's Microdomain

  • Set of basic object types (the letters a-z).
  • A basic relation between pairs of object types (successor-predecessor, e.g. {a,b}, {f,g}, {y,z} -- but not {z,a}).
  • A one dimensional ``space'' where structures (strings of object tokens can exist, e.g. abc, aabd, mrrjjj. Copycat can take account of the length of (short) strings.
  • The concepts of sameness and opposite (e.g. successor/predecessor,
    leftmost/rightmost).

The Slipnet: 1

  • Contains the permanent concepts (the 26 letters, also letter, predecessor, successor, left, right, direction, group, group length, 1, 2, 3, sameness, opposite...). Total of around 60.
  • ``Core'' of each concept represented by a node, each conceptual relationship by a link, with a length representing ``conceptual distance''.
  • Nodes acquire, spread and lose activation (relevance to the current situation); links adjust their lengths.
  • Node and its ``halo'' of related nodes correspond to a concept.

The Slipnet: 2

  • Each node has a conceptual depth -- generality, abstractness, distance from direct perception. Once perceived, ``deeper'' concepts have more influence; activation decays more slowly.
  • Links have ``labels'' which are themselves nodes (e.g. opposite; activation of a label node shortens the links with that label.
  • ``Slippability'' between concepts depends on existence of discrete ``cores'' (which do not exist in distributed connectionist models).

The Workspace: 1

  • Initially, unconnected pieces of raw data representing the situation.
  • Multiple independent perceptual structures built (and dismantled) in parallel by ``codelets'', until a coherent viewpoint emerges.
  • Probability an object will receive attention depends on its salience -- a function of its importance and unhappiness.
  • Pairs of neighboring objects in the same string scanned for relationships which can produce bonds. Sets of bonded objects can form ``groups'' (higher-level objects).

The Workspace: 2

  • Pairs of objects in different strings can be linked by bridges or correspondences, gradually building up a coherent mapping.
  • Identity mappings always help formation of a bridge; conceptual slippages have to overcome resistance.
  • As Workspace evolves, pressure for a consistent viewpoint increases.
  • Events in Workspace alter activations in Slipnet, which react on Workspace...
  • General biases towards deep concepts and consistency give Copycat a ``goal-oriented'' quality despite decentralization.

The Coderack

  • Codelets do all the scanning, bonding, bridging, grouping...
  • Scout codelets estimate promise of actions: all they do is create further codelets. Effector codelets attach descriptions to objects, or create/destroy bonds/groups/bridges.
  • Created codelets placed in Coderack, assigned an urgency determining probability of being selected to be run and removed.
  • There are bottom-up codelets, continually being added; top-down ones, placed by highly activated Slipnet nodes; follow-up codelets placed by earlier codelets.

Emergence of Fluidity

  • H/M compare Copycat's operations to what goes on inside a cell: no centre of control, but interlocking feedback loops.
  • Copycat architecture allows many ``pressures'' to coexist, overlap, compete and cooperate.
  • Use of scout codelets allows exploration of possibilities in a parallel terraced scan.
  • Initial ``discoveries'' are local; scale of actions gradually increases.
  • ``Temperature'' variable, reflecting how orderly Workspace is, controls degree of randomness used. Final temperature reflects solution quality.

Overall Trends During Run

  • Slipnet: Activation moves from ``shallower'' to ``deeper'' concepts, and increasingly clusters into themes of closely-related, deep concepts.
  • Workspace: Shift from many local, unrelated objects toward a few global, coherent structures.
  • Coderack: Initially, only bottom-up codelets exist. Top-down codelets gradually come to dominate as a run proceeds.
  • Processing: shifts from parallel towards serial, bottom-up towards top-down, nondeterministic towards deterministic.

Copycat's Answers to Problem 4

1000 runs of abc abd, mrrjjj ?

  • 203: mrrjjk Av. Temp: 50
  • 45: mrrjkk Av. Temp: 46
  • 39: mrrjjjj Av. Temp: 20
  • 6: mrrddd Av. Temp: 46
  • 2: mrrjjd Av. Temp: 61

Robustness and Elegance

  • Copycat has the potential to produce ``bad'' or ``weird'' answers, but avoids them most of the time -- H/M regard this as a demonstration of its robustness.
  • The potential to produce ``bad'' answers appears essential if it is to be able to produce impressive ``elegant'' ones (like mrrjjjj in problem 4).
  • If temperature is ``clamped'' (forced to remain constant) it seldom finds what Hofstadter considers the most ``elegant'' answers.
  • Testing on families of related problems can be seen as ``miniature Turing tests''.

Can Copycat ``Scale Up''?

Remains to be proved, but H/M claim that none of the key ideas depend on its small domain, and that the way in which concepts initially disregarded can move first to the periphery, then to the centre of attention, allowing ``Dynamic emergence of unpredictable objects and pathways'' to occur, will permit domains of much greater size to be dealt with.
