Assessment: 2-hour exam, three sections with two questions each, answer one from each section.

Natural and Artificial Intelligence

Structure of this part of the module:

  • Introduction: lecture 1.
  • Two Approaches to Discovery: lectures 2-4.
    • Herbert Simon and GOFAI: lecture 2-3.
    • Doug Hofstadter and FARG: lecture 4.
  • Biologically-inspired computing: lectures 5-8.
    • Neural networks: lectures 5-6.
    • Genetic algorithms: lecture 7.
    • Ant colonies: lecture 8.

Lecture 1: Introduction

  • How does AI compare with NI, how does study of the two interact?
  • What computers can and can't do. Deep Blue and the Turing test. Some rash claims.
  • Formal, expert and mundane tasks. Microworlds.
  • Whether (and if so how) AI is possible remain open questions.

Lecture 2: GOFAI/Simon

  • GOFAI and the ``Physical Symbol System Hypothesis''.
  • Simon's view of the mind; importance of the environment, nature of long-term memory, nature of learning.
  • Simon's stress on modelling human behaviour.
  • GPS: separation of domain-specific knowledge and problem-solving strategy.

Lecture 3: Simon on Discovery

  • Simon's view of scientific discovery, claims for his and other programs.
  • Problem-solving as selective search.
  • Well-structured and ill-structured problems.
  • BACON, GLAUBER, STAHL, DALTON. Integrated discovery system?
  • Hofstadter's critique.
  • Comparison: DENDRAL, META-DENDRAL.

Lecture 4: Hofstadter on Discovery

  • Simon and Hofstadter.
  • Hofstadter's central concerns.
  • Example programs.
  • Copycat
    • Overview
    • Domain and examples.
    • Structure.
    • Performance.

Lecture 5: Neural Networks Overview

  • Symbolic or subsymbolic; roots of connectionism.
  • Real and artificial neural networks.
  • Perceptrons.
  • The neural net revivial: backpropagation, Hopfield networks, Boltzmann machines, Kohonen maps, Jordan networks.
  • Example domain: speech processing.
  • Advantages and disadvantages of NNs.

Lecture 6: Arbib

  • Structural and functional hierarchies in the brain. Neurons, schemas.
  • Schema-level and neural-network models. Example -- the computational frog.
  • Frogs and robots.
  • Principles: cooperative computation, evolution and modulation, interaction of partial representations.
  • Instinctive to reflective.
  • Biology and technology.

Lecture 7: Genetic Algorithms

  • Evolutionary computation in general. Different approaches.
  • Genetic algorithm example: initializing population, selection, recombination, evolution.
  • Fundamental theorem of genetic algorithms.
  • Recombination, mutation, other ``operators''.
  • Application domains: timetabling, scheduling, robot control, design...
  • Classifier systems: rules, message list, bucket brigade, role of GAs, applications.
  • Evolutionary machine learning prospects.

Lecture 8: Ant Colonies

  • Intelligence is social.
  • Real ants: pheromones and stigmergy. Finding food sources and shortest routes.
  • Ant System: distributed optimization. Difference between ant-based and other multi-agent systems. Different algorithms for travelling salesman problem: ANT-quantity, ANT-density,ANT-cycle. Effect of using more ants.
  • ABC: Ruud Schoonderwoerd's ant-based control system for load-balancing in telecoms networks. Why such systems are needed. Network simulation and how ants find routes. Ants versus load agents.

The Exam

  • You will have to choose one out of two questions on this part of the module.
  • The exam may draw on anything in the slides, anything discussed in the lectures, and anything in the handouts.
  • Questions will be multi-part, and will attempt to test both your knowledge of the material in the lectures and handouts, and your ability to think about and use this material intelligently.
  • The latter might require you (say) to compare and contrast two programs or bodies of work, or to sketch a possible method of tackling some problem in the spirit of one of the approaches described.

One Possible Revision Strategy

  • Read through slides, together with any notes you made, quickly: to fix overall ``shape'' of this part of the module in your mind.
  • Read again, more slowly, noting down points not clear to you.
  • Read the handouts, particularly if you didn't at the time.
  • Discuss points that puzzle you with friends.
  • Using one-slide summaries given here as ``cues'', try to rmemember (maybe write down) as much as you can about each lecture (and handout if any).

Further Assistance

  • I'll be ready to answer, by email, brief, clear, specific questions about points that still puzzle you (if I know the answer).
  • I will not be able to supply long re-explanations.
  • Nor will I give any hints about what is or is not covered in the exam.
