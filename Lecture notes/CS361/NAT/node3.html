CS36110, Part 1, Lecture 3:

Scientific Discovery --

BACON and other Programs

Simon's Claims

  • Scientific discovery can be explained in terms of general features of human thinking or problem-solving, which Simon considers are quite well understood.
  • ``Computer programs exist today that, given the same initial conditions that confronted certain human scientists, remake the discoveries the scientists made.''
  • At least one program (DENDRAL) has made original scientific discoveries. There is no reason why others should not do the same.

Simon On Scientific Discovery

  • Simon believes working scientists spend more time on scientific discovery (looking for regularities in data and trying to find some theory explaining them), than in testing or comparing theories.
  • He sees scientific discovery as fundamentally the same as other forms of problem-solving, involving no ``special mechanisms'' of insight or inspiration.
  • His approach to understanding the process of scientific research involves breaking it down into subprocesses, making AI models of the parts, then integrating these models.

Simon's On Problem Solving

  • Most problem solving involves selective search through large spaces of possibilities.
  • Both domain-specific and general heuristics are used: human beings fall back on general heuristics (``weak methods'') when domain-specific ones are unavailable or fail.
  • The most important heuristic is means-end analysis. Effectiveness depends on ability to recognise features of current state, and recall relevant operators for reducing the difference between it and the goal state.
  • The domain-specific (recognition and recall) knowledge distinguishing experts from novices is stored in the form of productions (condition-action rules).

Search Spaces

  • A search space includes:
    • A set of nodes or states, including an initial node, and one or more goal nodes.
    • A set of operators, used in order to move from the current node to a neighbouring one.
  • The search may be:
    • Exhaustive: all nodes are examined, using procedures like depth-first or breadth-first search.
    • Heuristic: guided by some way of estimating which node should be ``expanded'' next, and/or which operator should be used.

How Productions Guide Search

  • A production fires when its condition is matched by contents of short-term memory and/or features of current environment.
  • Its action is then to alter the contents of short-term memory.
  • An important way of doing this is to recall information from long-term memory.
  • This information can then be used in guiding a search process.

Well-Structured Problems

1.
Effective test of proposed solutions.
2.
At least one problem-space where all ``considerable'' states can be represented.
3.
At least one problem-space where all ``considerable''moves can be represented.
4.
Any relevant knowledge that can be acquired can be represented in terms of one or more problem-spaces.
5.
If action on the external world is involved, effects of actions are accurately known.
6.
Only ``reasonable'' amounts of computation are required.

Alternatives To Simple Search

Applicability of search to ``ill-structured'' real-world problems can be challenged. Simon suggests various extensions/modifications including:

  • Planning: try to find some intermediate nodes on the way from the initial node to a goal node.
  • Create a less abstract version of the problem and use that to guide search (e.g., using a diagram to guide a search for a geometric proof).
  • Put information gathered at one node onto a ``blackboard'' so it can be used elsewhere. Taking this as far as possible produces a constraint-based approach, where nodes are states of knowledge rather than of the world.

Well-Structured/Ill-Structured

Simon also argues that the distinction between well-structured and ill-structured problems is one of degree, not qualitative.

  • Apparently well-structured problems (e.g. chess), do not meet the conditions given: we can't normally be sure a proposed move wins (too much computation), and we can't be sure what the consequences of a move will be (what the opponent will do). The space to be searched is different every move.
  • Conversely, a problem like designing a house or ship looks ill-structured, but is decomposed into a series of more-or-less well-structured problems.

BACON

  • BACON is given data involving one or more ``independent variables'' and one ``dependent variable'' (e.g. temperature, quantity of gas, pressure as i.v.s, volume as d.v.).
  • ``Searches'' in 2 ``spaces'': space of data and space of laws: ``the system's search for laws is embedded within its search for data.''.
  • The ``search'' in the ``space of data'', however, is trivial: the values the i.v.s can take are given by the user, and all nodes are visited in a fixed order.
  • User provides ``information about the form that plausible laws may take.''

BACON: Searching For Laws

1.
Keeping all i.v.s but one fixed, BACON searches for a ``set of parameters'' relating values of the the last i.v. to those of the d.v.. ``Best'' set is the one that ``maximally predicts'' the observed data.
2.
Search results in a ``law'', stored for future use if there ismore than 1 i.v.
3.
Steps (1) and (2) repeated for each possible value of another i.v. The first-level ``laws'' are then used to search for a second-level law, relating two i.v.s to the d.v.
4.
This process is continued through as many levels as there are i.v.s.
5.
BACON can also handle non-numerical i.v.s (e.g. different substances).

GLAUBER

Aimed at discovering qualitative rather than quantitative relations: simulating discovery of classes of chemical substances such as acids, alkalis and salts. Viewed as a search program:

  • Initial state: ``facts'' about particular chemicals. Goal state: definitions of ``classes'' of these chemicals, with ``laws'' relating them.
  • Operators: Form-law -- defines a class and substitutes it into ``facts''; Determine-quantifier -- specifies whether the law applies to all members of the class(es) concerned, or just to some.
  • ``Best-first'' search with no backtracking. Form-law selects object occurring in the most ``analogous facts''; user determines how ``cautious'' Determine-quantifier is.

STAHL

Aimed at determining the components of substances. Viewed as a search program:

  • Initial state: list of reactions relating substances. Goal state: components of each compound substance.
  • Example operators+heuristics:
    • Infer-composition. If A and B react to form C, or if C decomposes into A and B, then infer that C is composed of A and B.
    • Identify-compounds. If A is composed of C and D, and B is composed of C and D, then identify A with B.
  • Depth-first search without backtracking.

DALTON

``Follows on'' from STAHL (accpets as input the type of information output by STAHL). Viewed as a search program:

  • Initial state: list of reactions, and components of substances involved. Goal state: Model of each reaction, specifying number of molecules of each substance, number of ``particles'' (atoms) in each type of molecule.
  • Operators: Determine-molecules hypothesizes number of molecules of a particular type involved. Determine-atoms hypothesizes number of atoms of a particular type in a particular molecule. Conserve-particles ensures number of atoms of each type is unchanged by the reaction.

Towards An Integrated Discovery System?

``To the extent that it can be achieved, an integrated discovery system would be much less susceptible to the criticism that one is `building in discoveries' by providing the necessary inputs.''

Hofstadter's Critique

  • Hofstadter takes BACON as exemplifying serious flaws in most GOFAI research, which bypasses problems such as the integration of perception and cognition, and the ability to shift flexibly between representations of a situation.
  • Claims BACON and Simon's other programs are fed data they need ``predigested'', unlike scientists being ``simulated''.
  • Qin and Simon found university students, starting with the same data as BACON, often made the same ``discoveries'' within an hour. This shows BACON has little relation to real scientific discovery!
  • Easy to be fooled into thinking an AI program understands more than it does.

Comparison: DENDRAL

  • Developed by E. Feigenbaum and others from 1965, first publication 1971.
  • Domain: enumerating structures of organic molecules, given data from spectrometers, plus user-supplied constraints.
  • Switch to (situation-action) rule-based approach 1967. Rule has to capture a meaningful chunk of knowledge.
  • Better than human experts within certain narrow classes of molecules, but much less flexible. Regularly used by Stanford University chemists.

DENDRAL's Approach

  • Generate all topologically possible structures (using a subprogram called CONGEN)...
  • within constraints set by the user or a built-in planning process which favours the most plausible structures.
  • Then test the proposed structures by predicting the instrument data.

META-DENDRAL

META-DENDRAL inferred rules of mass spectrometry, for possible use by DENDRAL.

  • Input: spectrometer data from known molecules.
  • Output: set of fragmentation rules discovered, plus summary of evidence for and against each rule.
  • Produced rule-sets of comparable quality to those produced by consultation with experts.
